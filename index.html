You are an Auditory learner!

Auditory

If you are an auditory learner, you learn by hearing and listening. You understand and remember things you have heard. You store information by the way it sounds, and you have an easier time understanding spoken instructions than written ones. You often learn by reading out loud because you have to hear it or speak it in order to know it.

As an auditory learner, you probably hum or talk to yourself or others if you become bored. People may think you are not paying attention, even though you may be hearing and understanding everything being said.

Here are some things that auditory learners like you can do to learn better.

  • Sit where you can hear.
  • Have your hearing checked on a regular basis.
  • Use flashcards to learn new words; read them out loud.
  • Read stories, assignments, or directions out loud.
  • Record yourself spelling words and then listen to the recording.
  • Have test questions read to you out loud.
  • Study new material by reading it out loud.
Remember that you need to hear things, not just see things, in order to learn well.

Project Ideas

Overview

The project idea is to develop a website for end-users and admins using products with limited or poor support. A self-help/peer-help documentation repository and forum. The site would contain templates and instructions on setting up a how-to, and threads for different products and problems, along with statistics on people’s overall experience with products – is it worth it to keep going with it, or should you cut your losses?

It could be white-labelled for developers just setting out who don’t yet have their documentation processes up to scratch. Ideally, developers would not like to have their produce feature on such a site (hence “white-label”), but if they did, would be able to use it to gain insights into their users’ experiences.

Motivation

In my job as inhouse IT at a local disability service provider, I work with a client management system, let’s call it “Trouble”.

Trouble is built for a niche market and is only targeted for Australian datasets. The product has grown in features and complexity to keep up with changing needs, however, the developers have poorly documented their changes or updated the manuals and haven’t tested thoroughly before release. We find the support team a nightmare to work with their go-to response being to blame us not doing it right. I found myself developing a manual for my end-users to use and considering selling it to the developers.

The support provided is sub-par, but the system is so very deeply embedded in the business that moving on would be almost catastrophic.

Description

My team and I wonder at how other users of Trouble deal with their issues. Do they just work around the bugs? Do they get better support because they offer more revenue? We know why there’s no forums. The program is targeted at such a niche market, one that’s not generally into developing forums, and the system is sold directly to business through the developer, so there are no partners who may be in a position to get the ball rolling.

I would like to develop a website for a sort of self-help/peer-help site where businesses who could offer advice or solutions for issues in products with similar issues as Trouble. Where there should be better support and documentation, but there just isn’t.

The site would be a platform for collaborative assistance with documentation developed by the consumers of products; things like pesky environment settings that can break the whole program, or the best way to set up security settings so that your data is secure from inexperienced end users. It could be white-labelled for developers just setting out who don’t yet have their documentation processes up to scratch. Ideally, developers would not like to have their produce feature on such a site (hence “white-label”), but if they did, would be able to use it to gain insights into their users’ experiences and even into ways of using their products that they had not investigated.

Tools and Technologies

Skills Required

The skills required would be full stack website development, i.e. html, css, php/js, databasing, maybe SQL, graphic design, testing, security. There would be need for experience with developing documentation, putting together forum type interactivity, developing templates, moderating posts.

Outcome

If this project were to be successful, businesses feeling cornered by a product with poor support since so mush of their business involves is use would be able to seek support from consumers of the same product who may have experienced similar issues.

From the other side, developers would have the opportunity to assess the experiences of their customers, or customers of competing companies and to improve upon their services and products accordingly. It would also be possible for developers of new products to quickly and easily get a functional support system for their users in place.
